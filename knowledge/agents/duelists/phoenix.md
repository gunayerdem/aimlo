# AGENT: Phoenix

## 1. Role Identity
- Phoenix is the self-sustaining duelist who creates his own opportunities through flashes, a fire wall, and a molly that heals him, making him the most independent entry fragger in the roster.
- Pick Phoenix when the team needs a duelist who can entry without relying heavily on team utility. Choose Phoenix over Jett when the team lacks flashes, or when self-healing sustain matters more than repositioning ability.
- Phoenix's core identity is the self-sufficient entry fragger who generates his own space through flash discipline, heals off his own utility, and uses Run It Back to take aggressive risks without permanent consequences.

## 2. Core Responsibilities
- **Attack-side duties:** Flash into contested angles and swing behind the flash. Use Blaze (fire wall) to cut off defender sightlines and create safe crossing routes. Entry site with Run It Back active to gather information and get risk-free kills. Use Hot Hands (molly) to clear corners or heal between fights.
- **Defense-side duties:** Hold aggressive off-angles using flashes to win peeks. Use fire wall to delay pushes and buy time for rotates. Use Run It Back for aggressive retake plays. Self-heal with molly during extended holds.
- **What the team expects:** Phoenix should be generating first contact through his own flashes. The team expects him to entry independently, self-heal to stay in the fight longer, and use ult aggressively for information or high-risk peeks.

## 3. Common Mistakes
1. **Flashing teammates instead of enemies** — Phoenix's Curveball has a wide flash radius and bends around corners. Throwing it without calling direction or without understanding where teammates are positioned blinds allies and causes lost fights.
2. **Using Run It Back passively** — Players activate ult and then play cautiously, wasting the entire purpose. Ult should enable the most aggressive play possible — wide swings, deep pushes, and information gathering.
3. **Healing in unsafe positions** — Standing in Hot Hands to heal while exposed to angles creates easy kills for enemies. Players must heal behind cover or inside Blaze wall safety.
4. **Throwing Blaze wall for self-healing instead of team utility** — The fire wall is most valuable as a sightline blocker. Using it only to stand in for healing wastes its primary function as a space-creating tool.
5. **Flashing then not swinging** — The flash is wasted if Phoenix does not immediately peek behind it. Curveball has a short blind duration, so the swing must be instant.
6. **Predictable flash timing** — Using Curveball at the exact same angle and timing every round allows enemies to simply turn away. Vary flash side (left/right curve) and timing.
7. **Ult placement in dangerous positions** — Run It Back returns Phoenix to the activation point. If enemies push and hold that position, he dies immediately upon return. Place ult in safe, cleared locations.

## 4. Pattern -> Meaning
IF: Player has low first-kill rate despite using flashes frequently
MEANING: They are likely not swinging behind their own flash, or the flash is not connecting due to predictable angles
FIX: Drill the flash-swing combo. The swing should begin before the flash pops, not after. Review flash angles for variety.

IF: Player frequently dies immediately after Run It Back ends
MEANING: The ult spawn point is in an unsafe location that enemies are pushing or holding
FIX: Coach safer ult placement. The ult spot should be behind cover with a teammate nearby to protect it, or in an area already cleared.

IF: Player has high damage output but low heal usage
MEANING: They are not utilizing Hot Hands for sustain between fights, reducing their longevity in rounds
FIX: Remind the player that Phoenix's healing is free value. After any fight, drop Hot Hands behind cover. Even 30 HP recovered can win the next duel.

IF: Player's teammates report being flashed frequently
MEANING: Flash discipline is poor, either not calling flash direction or not checking teammate positions
FIX: Teach directional flash calls ("flashing left/right") and timing coordination. Phoenix must always know where his team is before throwing Curveball.

IF: Player gets multi-kills during ult but team loses the round
MEANING: The ult is being used for ego plays instead of strategic entries. Kills during ult that do not translate to site control are wasted.
FIX: Coach ult usage as a site-entry tool, not a frag-hunting tool. The goal of Run It Back is to clear two to three angles and create space the team can capitalize on.

## 5. Map Interactions
- **Strong maps:** Ascent (B Main flash entries are strong, mid control with wall and flash combo), Bind (tight corridors reward flash aggression, Hookah flash entries), Split (A Main and B Heaven flash-peeks, self-sustain matters in prolonged site fights).
- **Weak maps:** Breeze (long sightlines reduce flash value, wide open spaces make fire wall less impactful), Icebox (vertical fighting reduces ground-level flash effectiveness, large sites dilute wall value).
- **Position-specific:** On Ascent, flashing out B Main and immediately swinging with fire wall across Market is a strong entry pattern. On Bind, Hookah flash-peek into Short creates consistent first contact opportunities.

## 6. Matchup Notes
- **Struggles against:** Skye (her flash can re-flash Phoenix mid-swing, her heal competes with his sustain advantage), KAY/O (suppression disables Phoenix's entire self-sufficient kit), Chamber (punishes Phoenix's predictable flash-swing timing with fast reactions).
- **Counters:** Agents who rely on holding static positions. Phoenix's flash forces them off angles. Effective against Cypher (flash past tripwires), and against Sage (fire wall can melt Barrier Orb).
- **Adaptation:** Against KAY/O, play further from his suppression radius and time entries after his knife expires. Against Skye, bait her flash before committing to the swing. Against Chamber, vary flash timing so the headhunter cannot pre-aim the swing angle.

## 7. What To Tell The Player
### When performing well
**Player Action:** You swing within 0.3 seconds of every Curveball pop, alternate left-curve and right-curve between rounds, and use Hot Hands to heal to full HP between each duel.
**Enemy Perception:** Defenders track your Curveball direction from the previous round and prepare to turn that way. When you switch curve direction, their turn goes the wrong way and they eat the full 1.1-second blind.
**Enemy Reaction:** They are forced to guess which direction to turn before the flash pops. Wrong guesses mean 1.1 seconds of full blindness. Right guesses still cost 0.3-0.5 seconds of reaction time — and you are already swinging during that window.
**Exploit Window:** The 0.3-1.1 second window between Curveball pop and enemy vision recovery is your guaranteed first-shot opportunity. With alternating curve directions, even players who guess correctly lose 0.3 seconds — enough for you to land the opening shot.
**Repeat Strategy:** Round 1: right-curve flash, swing immediately. Round 2: left-curve same angle. Round 3: right-curve from a different position. Between every kill, drop Hot Hands behind cover and heal to full before the next fight. Enter each successive duel at 150 HP while defenders sit at 100 or less.
**Enemy Adaptation:** After 4-5 rounds, defenders will pre-turn away from both directions (playing with back turned), hold deeper positions outside flash range, or push aggressively before you can flash.
**Counter-Adaptation:** When they pre-turn: delay your peek by 0.5 seconds so they turn back thinking the flash failed, then catch them mid-rotation. When they hold deep: use Blaze wall to cut off their deep position and force them into flash range. When they push you: drop Hot Hands in the doorway as area denial and fight from behind the fire.
### When struggling
"You are not swinging behind your own flash. Throw the Curveball and immediately wide-peek. If you flash and wait, the blind expires before you arrive."
### When being predictable
"They are turning away from your flash every time. Mix up left and right curves, or delay the flash by half a second to catch them mid-turn."

## 8. Rank Modulation
### Low Elo (Iron-Silver)
Focus on not flashing teammates. Learn which direction Curveball bends and practice calling it out. Always use Hot Hands to heal after fights. Use Run It Back every round it is available — do not save it.
### Mid Elo (Gold-Diamond)
Develop the flash-swing combo as an instant reflex. Learn fire wall placements that block key sightlines on two to three maps. Start placing ult in safe positions with teammate support. Manage healing versus utility usage for Hot Hands.
### High Elo (Ascendant-Immortal)
Master varied flash timing to prevent enemies from turning. Coordinate ult usage with team executes for maximum impact. Use fire wall creatively — not just for blocking sightlines but for forcing enemy repositions.
### Elite (Radiant)
Focus on round-by-round flash adaptation based on how enemies respond. Optimize ult placement for maximum information while maintaining safety. Use self-healing to maintain economic advantage over rounds — staying alive with 50 HP and healing is worth more than dying and rebuying.
