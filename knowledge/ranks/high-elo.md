# High Elo Coaching Profile — Ascendant, Immortal

## Coaching Tone
Analytical and precise. These players understand the game deeply. They do not need motivation or basics — they need targeted, data-driven observations about specific patterns they may not see themselves. Speak like an analyst reviewing film, not a teacher explaining concepts. Reference specific rounds, specific timings, and specific positions. Vague feedback is useless at this level.

## Priority Coaching Areas

### Timing Reads
High-elo games are decided by timing. When to peek, when to rotate, when to use utility — all of these have optimal windows that shift based on enemy behavior. Coaching should identify timing patterns: "The enemy Jett peeks A long at 1:25 three rounds in a row. Pre-aim that angle at 1:24 with a flash ready." Specific round references and timestamps make feedback actionable.

### Space Control
The concept of controlling map space without committing to a fight is what separates Immortal from Diamond. Coaching should focus on how much of the map the player controls at any given moment. Utility used for space denial (not for kills) is the mark of high-level play. A smoke that blocks a sightline for 15 seconds is more valuable than a smoke that gets a kill once.

### Enemy Pattern Exploitation
At this level, both sides are reading each other. Coaching should explicitly name the enemy patterns observed: "Their Omen smokes mid every gun round at 1:20. Push through at 1:18 before the smoke lands." Then coach the player to deliberately break their own patterns to avoid being read in return.

### Economy Optimization
High-elo players buy correctly but may not optimize. Light shield + Spectre on a half-buy round can be upgraded to a strategic force with specific utility. Coach economy in terms of round-win probability: "A force here with 3 Spectres gives you 40% win chance. A full save gives you 95% on the next gun round. The math favors saving." Make economy decisions feel calculated, not habitual.

## Common Mistakes at This Level

- **Autopiloting**: Running the same default every round without adapting to what the enemy showed in the previous round. High-elo players can auto-execute strategies so smoothly that they stop thinking about whether the strategy is correct. Coach active mid-round decision-making.
- **Not adapting mid-game**: Winning the first half with a strategy and then running it unchanged in the second half, even when the enemy has adjusted. Coach halftime resets: "What did they change? What should we change in response?"
- **Overaggression in winnable rounds**: Taking a fight in a 4v2 that turns it into a 3v2. The round was already won — the aggressive peek added risk with no reward. Coach round-state awareness: "You had numbers. Play time, not fights."
- **Under-utilizing information**: Getting info (drone, recon, etc.) but not acting on it quickly enough. The info decays in 3-5 seconds. If the team does not capitalize on fresh info, the play was wasted.
- **Defaulting to mechanical solutions**: When a smart play fails, reverting to "I'll just out-aim them." At Immortal, opponents aim well too. The correct adjustment is usually tactical, not mechanical.

## What to Emphasize

The core message: **decision-making quality over mechanical performance.** At this level, aim difference between players is marginal. The player who gathers better information, processes it faster, and commits at the right moment wins. Every coaching point should reinforce the value of thinking before acting: "What information do you have? What does it suggest? What is the highest-percentage play?"

## Interpreting Death Patterns

When a high-elo player dies repeatedly at the same position or to the same player, the cause is almost always **being read by the opponent.** The player's position was correct in isolation but has become predictable through repetition. The coaching response must address anti-read: "You have been holding A short for 4 rounds. The enemy Raze is pre-nading it now. Hold off-site for one round to reset their read, then return to A short when they stop checking."

Deaths in clutch situations (1vX) at this level are worth analyzing for decision quality, not outcome. A correct decision that lost to a headshot is not a mistake. A bad decision that worked because the enemy whiffed is still a mistake. Coach the process, not the result.

If the player dies repeatedly in the first 20 seconds of a round, they are over-committing on early aggression. The fix is not "be less aggressive" but "gather 5 seconds of info before committing to the aggression."
