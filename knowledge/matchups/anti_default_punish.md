# MATCHUP: Anti-Default Punish

## Interaction Identity
A default is the disciplined attack: the team spreads across the map, gathers information, and commits to a site only when they have confirmed an advantage. Anti-default punish is the defensive response that exploits the weakness of this patience — aggression. Defaults are vulnerable to early pushes, lurk hunting, and timed aggression that disrupts the information-gathering phase. This matchup is about tempo: the default team wants to slow the game down and play on full information. The anti-default team wants to speed it up and force decisions before information is available.

## Common Punish Patterns
**Default team succeeding**: Map control is established methodically. Information agents scan both sites and identify the weaker defense. The team commits only when they have a numbers or utility advantage. Lurkers punish over-rotations. The default pace drains the defenders' patience, causing them to over-peek or rotate too early.

**Anti-default team punishing**: Early aggression into the default spread catches isolated attackers before they group. Aggressive mid-control forces the default team to fight for map space they expected to take for free. Fast rotations that arrive before the default team commits punish the slow execute. Utility that clears common info-gathering positions (Sova dart at default drone spots, Fade haunt at common lurk spots) denies the information the default needs.

## What Repeated Failure Means
If **anti-default aggression** keeps failing, the defenders are pushing individually and getting picked off by the spread-out attackers. Anti-default requires coordinated aggression, not solo hero plays. The defenders are feeding kills instead of disrupting.

If the **default** keeps getting disrupted, the team is spreading too thin (each player is isolated and easily picked) or they are too slow to commit when they have the information (giving defenders time to re-stack). The default needs faster commitment once the advantage is identified.

## What AIMLO Should Say
### When player is on the punished side
**Default getting punished by aggression**: "Your default is too passive. The enemy is pushing into your spread before you can group up. Either commit faster once you have info, or leave someone watching the aggressive push paths so you do not get picked one by one."

**Anti-default aggression failing**: "You are pushing alone and dying to their spread. Anti-default aggression needs to be coordinated — two players push together into one of their information positions. If you push solo, the default team trades you for free."

### When player is on the punishing side
**Anti-default disrupting successfully**: "Your early aggression is breaking their game plan. Keep pushing their info-gathering spots in pairs and forcing them to fight before they are ready."

**Default controlling the round**: "Your patience is paying off. You are finding the weak defense every round and committing at the right time. Stay disciplined — the moment you commit without info, you are guessing."

## Rank Modulation
**LOW**: Nobody runs defaults. Attacks are either rushes or random. This matchup is irrelevant. Coach basic attack structure first.

**MID**: Defaults start appearing but are loose and uncommitted. Anti-default aggression works easily because attackers are not positioned to punish it. Coach default structure for attackers and coordinated pushes for defenders.

**HIGH**: Defaults are disciplined and anti-default requires genuine coordination. Coach timing — when to push, where to push, and how to read the default's information-gathering phase.

**ELITE**: Both sides play the tempo game at a high level. The matchup is about reading the opponent's default pattern and timing aggression to disrupt the specific information they need. Coach pattern recognition across multiple rounds.
